## Learning Activities and Material

### Class Discussions

In the labs, you will not only be engaging in experimentation and data analysis, but also actively participate in group and class-wide discussions. Active participation in class discussion will help you develop a deeper understanding of key concepts introduced in this course. Don't be shy in providing your perspective to your peers: it will be tremendously helpful in helping the discussion move forward and help the class make sense of challenging ideas. All questions and responses will be valued in this class!

### Required Course Materials

- There is no required textbook or lab manual to purchase. All course material will be available on Canvas.
- You will need to bring a computing device with a keyboard to class (a laptop is best, but a tablet with a separate keyboard can also work). Your computer will need an internet connection (e.g. via UBCsecure) and a modern web browser (Chrome or Firefox preferred, Safari should work). There are a few loaner computers available if you occasionally need one (ask a TA or the instructor at the beginning of your lab session).
- All of our data analysis and lab notes will be kept in a private web-based interactive computing platform (JupyterHub), run by the Physics and Astronomy department. Details on how to access this platform are detailed in Lab 00. All data analysis and lab notes will be done on this platform in so-called "Jupyter notebooks".
- Jupyter notebook is a web-based interactive note-taking and computing application. You don't need to install any special software to use Jupyter notebooks in this course: all you need is an internet connection and a modern web browser.
- We will use Python, Markdown and LaTeX to interact in the Jupyter Notebook. Notebooks will be exported to HTML format (.html) for uploading to Canvas. All of these tools are commonly used in academia and industry alike. Understanding how to use these tools will come in handy in one way or another, either later in your studies or during your professional life. The sooner you start using them, the easier it will be!

Glossary:

- **HTML**: "HTML is the World Wide Web's core markup language. Originally, HTML was primarily designed as a language for semantically describing scientific documents." [[reference](https://html.spec.whatwg.org/#introduction)]
- **Jupyter Notebook**: "The Jupyter Notebook is a web-based interactive computing platform. The notebook combines live code, equations, narrative text, visualizations, ..." [[reference](https://jupyter.org/)]
- **JupyterHub**: "A multi-user version of the notebook designed for companies, classrooms and research labs" [[reference](https://jupyter.org/hub)]
- **LaTeX**: "LaTeX is a high-quality typesetting system; it includes features designed for the production of technical and scientific documentation." [[reference](https://www.latex-project.org/)]
- **Markdown**: "Markdown is a lightweight markup language that you can use to add formatting elements to plaintext text documents." [[reference](https://www.markdownguide.org/)]
- **Python**: "Python is a programming language that lets you work more quickly and integrate your systems more effectively." [[reference](https://www.python.org/)]

**Prelabs:** Prelab assignments are graded for effort-based completion. Prelabs are the primary place where you will learn and practice new coding and data analysis skills. Thus, completing the prelab ahead of your in-person lab session is critical for success in this course. Your Lab 00 submission is considered a prelab for the purposes of how it contributes to your overall grade.

**Lab work:** There are two categories of lab in this course, **Foundations Labs** and **Investigation Labs**, each of which is assessed differently. You will work closely with a partner to perform experiments and collect data. However, each partner will write their own notes in their own lab notebook and perform their own data analysis. Each partner turns in their own notebook at the end of each lab.

- **Foundations Labs** focus on skill development, such as learning new analysis approaches, learning to work with complex equipment and collecting pilot data to prepare you for an Investigation Lab in the subsequent week. These labs are assessed primarily through in-class checkpoint questions that focus on what and your understanding of what you have been recording in your lab notebook (80% of that lab's grade). We will also assess your submitted lab notebook for effort-based completion (20% of that lab's grade).
- Your first lab that will use checkpoint questions will be Lab 01. In the lab instructions you will find many prompts regarding specific things we want you to record or discuss in your lab notebook.
- An example prompt is "Which variables are under your control?" as part of your initial familiarization with your equipment. For a checkpoint question related to this prompt somebody on the teaching team would stop by your table and ask you this question, and then after you have had a chance to answer it, ask you to show us where in your lab notebook you have recorded your response to this prompt.
- These checkpoint questions are graded for engagement, meaning demonstrating appropriate effort in your verbal and written answers. If you have done so, you will earn full credit for that checkpoint question. If not, you can request that we return for reassessment later in the same lab period and if your verbal and written answers now demonstrate appropriate effort, you will earn 70% of the full credit grade for that checkpoint question.
- In a Foundations Lab we will typically ask you three checkpoint questions, but we will make it clear in the lab introduction how many to expect for that specific lab.
- **Investigation Labs** focus on developing your decision-making, analysis and other experimental skills. These labs are assessed primarily through a detailed rubric (provided ahead of time) and applied to your submitted lab notebook, which includes your data and data analysis. We will randomly choose one partner from each lab group for grading and assign this grade to both partners. Weeks where you receive a grade based on your notebook will carry twice the weight of those where your partner's notebook was graded or of a Foundations Lab.
- There may also be occasional in-class checkpoint questions as an additional individual assessment to an Investigation Lab. We will communicate our intention to do so clearly during that lab's introduction and will detail how the checkpoint questions will contribute to that lab's grade.

**Quizzes:** There will be two quizzes during the term, administered through the Computer-Based Testing Facility. The first quiz will be during the week of June 1 and the second quiz will be during the week of June 22. Practice quizzes, and further details about each quiz, will be provided at least one week in advance of that quiz.

### Lateness policies

Success in this class depends upon your active engagement and attention both in the prelabs and during the labs themselves. Coming prepared to your lab by taking the time to carefully complete and understand your prelab is critical to ensure you finish your lab on time. The following deadlines apply to all labs and prelabs:

- **Lab submissions** are due at the end of the lab period (2h50m after the start of the lab). We will provide a grace period of 10 minutes in case you have any technical issues related to submitting. Labs submitted after that (i.e. more than 3 hours after the start of your lab session) will have an automatic 10% penalty applied. Additionally, the lab submission assignment will no longer be available 4 hours after the start of your lab session.
- **Prelab submission** assignments will be open until the final teaching day of the term but any prelabs submitted after the start of the associated lab will have a 10% penalty applied. Lateness policies related to make-up labs are discussed in the next section.

**Late arrivals:** Since we work in teams, you are expected to arrive prepared and on time. If you are less than 15 minutes late we will ask you to join your partner or another group and to quickly catch up. If you are more than 15 minutes late, but less than an hour, you will be asked to complete the lab independently assuming sufficient equipment is available. If you arrive more than an hour late, we will treat it as a missed lab.

You will receive a grade of 0 for any lab that you do not attend, unless an in-term academic concession was granted for that lab.

## Course policies on academic integrity and plagiarism

> *Share ideas, not content*

Academic culture has its own set of norms and expectations surrounding academic integrity and plagiarism. As developing academics, we hope to help you navigate these norms and meet these expectations. UBC offers an [online guide](https://academicintegrity.ubc.ca/student-start/) to preventing unintentional plagiarism and organizing your writing.

Please visit the UBC calendar page on [academic misconduct](http://www.calendar.ubc.ca/Vancouver/index.cfm?tree=3,54,111,959) for a detailed discussion of academic integrity and plagiarism.

The following advice details how to avoid the most common academic misconduct issues that occur in this lab course, issues which may result in grades of zero on that lab and a letter to the Dean's office / head of program:

- **Prelab notebooks:** A major emphasis of this course is the development of Jupyter notebook skills and the prelabs are where you start this process. It is essential that everyone develop skills and confidence through practice during the prelab. As such, we expect that you will start each prelab with a blank template notebook (unless otherwise specified) and that all prelab-related work be done in your own notebook. This means that data, notes and code entry, as well as the creation of graphs was executed by you and that none of this work was copied from another source. There may be exceptions to this rule when prelab work involves working with your spreadsheet from the previous week, but these exceptions will be communicated clearly.
- **In-lab notebooks:** You are not permitted to use another student's prelab or in-lab notebook as the starting point for your own work. Copying and pasting from the work of another student counts as plagiarism. You can (and are encouraged to) copy and paste from your own work, and from templates provided to you in the course materials.
- **Sharing notebooks or spreadsheets:** You should never send a copy of your notebook or any of your work to another student (this is considered assisting in plagiarism and is academic misconduct) nor should you be in possession of copies of notebooks belonging to other students.
- **Text in notebooks:** Ideas can be shared, but written text should be your own words that represent your understanding (strategically changing the wording or paraphrasing your partner's work is still plagiarism). Discuss your ideas with your partner and lab-mates, but we should NEVER see anybody copying from or reading and paraphrasing the words from someone else's work.
- **Generative AI tools (e.g. ChatGPT).** You are responsible for the work you submit in your lab notebooks and prelabs. As such the use of tools such as ChatGPT and other similar tools, to write any part of your lab notes is **not acceptable and will be considered academic misconduct**. Your time in the lab is limited and is best spent performing experiments, analyzing data, reflecting on your results, and interacting with those around you, rather than interacting with a Gen AI tool. These tools are designed to produce the most statistically plausible text result and not necessarily the correct one. Your best resource in this class are your peers and the instructional team; we are here to help you succeed.
